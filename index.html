Sentry Mode

Default passive state. Minimum CPU/sensor polling to preserve battery. Monitors voice amplitude baseline and biometric baseline. Watching for threshold approach. Uses bandpass filter + RMS-to-dBFS conversion on audio. No events logged unless threshold approached.

Elevated Monitor

Activated when voice amplitude trends upward AND heart rate IBI shortens AND EDA shows rising conductance. Sampling rate increases. System enters pre-alert awareness. Pre-escalation window is captured with full biometric context for clinical reporting.

Alert State

Threshold exceeded. Haptic pattern fires immediately — specific vibration cadence distinguishable from notification vibrations. Optional LED indicator (red light, UTC prototype). Bidirectional: fires for voice too HIGH and voice too LOW (speak-up cue). Event timestamped. Regulation Beats prompt or box breathing cue can trigger.

Cooldown

Post-alert recovery. Sampling remains elevated. System monitors whether user has self-regulated. Transition back to Sentry when baseline restored. Cooldown duration is logged — this is a key clinical metric. Time-to-recovery is reportable in IEP documentation.

Audio Pipeline
Privacy-First Design — Critical to Understand The system NEVER records speech. It captures amplitude envelope only. A 10-second rolling buffer continuously overwrites itself. Only the processed dBFS value and timestamp are stored. No audio content ever persists on device or transfers to cloud.

Phase 2 — Personalized Baseline Learning

Ingests session history from cloud backend. Builds individual biometric baseline per student — their typical heart rate, voice amplitude range during calm, EDA baseline. Adjusts FSM thresholds automatically per child. Watch pulls updated parameters from cloud at session start. Phase 3 — Multi-Modal Pattern Recognition

Correlates voice amplitude + heart rate IBI + EDA together to identify each child's unique pre-escalation signature. No single signal is a reliable predictor. Phase 4 — Population Insights

Cross-student pattern analysis. Which intervention prompts correlate with faster recovery. What biometric signatures are most predictive across age groups. Research and outcomes layer that Pearson, school districts, and research institutions pay for separately.

Why The Clip-On Matters Technically

Acoustic Accuracy

A wrist microphone is 60–90cm from the mouth. A collar clip-on is 20–30cm. Signal-to-noise ratio improvement is significant in a classroom with 20+ students. Ambient noise filtering in the clip-on firmware means the dBFS value transmitted to the watch is cleaner — fewer false positives in the FSM.

Bidirectional Feedback

The clip-on supports BOTH thresholds — too loud (hypervocalization) AND too soft (hypophonia). This is patented as bidirectional monitoring. The watch-only microphone can detect too-loud but has limited sensitivity for too-soft at wrist distance. The clip-on enables both directions with equivalent accuracy.

Children Who Can't Wear Wristbands

Some neurodivergent children have tactile sensitivities and cannot tolerate wrist-worn devices. The clip-on module can be desk-mounted or worn on clothing without a wristband component. This expands the addressable population — critical for school deployment scale.

Patent Coverage

Claims 21–25 in the non-provisional specifically cover the dual-device architecture. The clip-on as a separate module with BLE transmission to a primary wrist device is a claimed innovation. No competitor has filed this combination for the neurodivergent voice monitoring use case.
